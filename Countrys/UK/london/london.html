London

London, the capital of the United Kingdom, is known for its rich history, culture and iconic landmarks. As one of the most visited cities in the world, London offers something for everyone to enjoy. Visitors can explore famous landmarks such as Big Ben and the Houses of Parliament, Buckingham Palace, and the Tower of London.

The city also boasts beautiful parks such as Hyde Park and St. James's Park, as well as world-renowned museums like the British Museum and the National Gallery. London's culinary scene is diverse, with a wide variety of traditional British dishes and international cuisine.

Shopping in London is a unique experience, with a wide range of independent boutiques and famous department stores such as Harrods and Selfridges. London is a perfect destination for travelers looking for a mix of culture, history, entertainment, and the best of British art and architecture.

1. Big Ben and the Houses of Parliament

Big Ben and the Houses of Parliament, also known as the Palace of Westminster, are one of the most iconic landmarks in London and a symbol of British democracy and history. The clock tower, Big Ben, is one of the most famous clocks in the world and the bell inside, known as Big Ben, is a famous symbol of London. But did you know that these landmarks have some interesting facts that you might not have heard before?


  • Big Ben is actually the name of the bell inside the clock tower, not the clock tower itself.

  • The Houses of Parliament were nearly destroyed in a fire in 1834, and were rebuilt in the Gothic Revival style that we see today.

  • The clock tower stands at a height of 316 feet, and is the third-tallest free-standing clock tower in the world.

2. Buckingham Palace

Buckingham Palace is the residence and administrative headquarters of the monarch of the United Kingdom. It is one of the most famous landmarks in London and is known for its grand architecture and opulent interiors. But did you know that the palace has some interesting facts that you might not have heard before?


  • The palace was originally built for the Duke of Buckingham in 1703.

  • The palace has 775 rooms, including 19 state rooms, 52 royal and guest bedrooms, 188 staff bedrooms, 92 offices, and 78 bathrooms.

  • The palace's famous Changing of the Guard ceremony takes place daily at 11:30 am.

  • The palace has its own post office, swimming pool, doctor's surgery, jeweler, and cinema.

  • The palace has its own lake, which is home to swans, ducks, and pelicans.

3. The British Museum

The British Museum is one of the oldest and most famous museums in the world, located in London, UK. It is known for its vast collection of art and artifacts from around the globe, spanning over 2 million years of human history. The museum's collection includes famous objects such as the Rosetta Stone and the Elgin Marbles. But did you know that the British Museum has some interesting facts that you might not have heard before?


  • The British Museum was officially opened in 1753.

  • The museum receives over 6 million visitors annually, making it one of the most visited museums in the world.

  • The museum's collection includes over 8 million objects, with only a small fraction on display at any given time.

  • The museum's Great Court, completed in 2000, features a stunning glass roof designed by British architect Norman Foster.

4. The London Eye

The London Eye is one of the most iconic landmarks in London and offers a breathtaking view of the city. The giant Ferris wheel stands 135 meters tall and is located on the South Bank of the River Thames. But did you know that the London Eye has some interesting facts that you might not have heard before?


  • The London Eye was officially opened in 2000 and has since become one of the most popular tourist attractions in London.

  • Each rotation on the London Eye takes approximately 30 minutes, giving you a chance to take in the panoramic views of the city.

  • The London Eye is made up of 32 capsules, each representing one of the London Boroughs, and can carry up to 800 people at a time.

  • The London Eye was originally intended to be a temporary attraction but it was so popular that it became a permanent fixture of London's skyline.

5. St. Paul's Cathedral

St. Paul's Cathedral is one of London's most iconic landmarks and a symbol of the city's rich history and cultural heritage. The grand baroque cathedral, designed by Sir Christopher Wren, has stood tall over the city for over 300 years and is a must-see destination for any visitor. But did you know that the St. Paul's Cathedral has some interesting facts that you might not have heard before?


  • The cathedral is the second-largest in the UK after Liverpool Cathedral.

  • The cathedral's dome is the second-largest brick dome in the world after St. Peter's Basilica in Rome.

  • The cathedral was used as a navigation point for pilots during WWII as it was one of the few buildings in London with a dome visible from the air.

  • The cathedral's Whispering Gallery is called so because sound travels around the dome in a unique way, allowing people to whisper in one corner and be heard clearly in the opposite corner.

8. Tower Bridge

Tower Bridge is one of the most iconic landmarks in London and is known for its unique design, featuring two towers connected by a walkway. It was built in 1894 and is now a grade I listed building. It offers spectacular views of the city and is a popular tourist destination.


  • The bridge's design was inspired by the nearby Tower of London, hence its name.

  • The bridge's walkway is actually a glass floor, providing a unique perspective of the River Thames below.

  • The bridge was the largest and most sophisticated bascule bridge of its time and continues to be a marvel of engineering today.

9. The Natural History Museum

The Natural History Museum is one of the most iconic and popular museums in London. It is known for its vast collection of natural specimens, including everything from dinosaurs to precious gemstones. The museum has something for everyone, from the curious kid to the science enthusiast. But did you know that the Natural History Museum has some interesting facts that you might not have heard before?


  • The Natural History Museum was officially opened in 1881.

  • The museum receives over 5 million visitors annually, making it one of the most visited museums in London.

  • The museum's collection includes over 80 million specimens, from fossils and bones to live insects and plants.

  • The museum's famous Diplodocus skeleton, known as Dippy, was on display for over 100 years before being replaced in 2017 by a Blue Whale skeleton.

  • The museum has an earthquake simulator that allows visitors to experience a simulated earthquake of up to 8.0 magnitude.

10. Camden Market

Camden Market is a vibrant and eclectic destination in London, known for its unique shopping experience and bohemian atmosphere. Visitors can find a wide range of items including vintage clothing, handmade crafts, and street food from around the world.


  • Camden Market first opened in the 1970s and has since become a popular tourist destination.

  • The market is made up of several different areas including Camden Stables Market, Camden Lock Market, and Camden Canal Market.

  • The market has a unique and alternative vibe, making it a favorite spot for fashion enthusiasts and music lovers alike.
